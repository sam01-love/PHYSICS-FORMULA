Classical Mechanics Kinematics · Dynamics · Energy · Momentum
Velocity
v = u + at
Final velocity from initial velocity under constant acceleration.
v = final velocityu = initial velocitya = accelerationt = time
Displacement
s = ut + ½at²
Distance covered under uniform acceleration from rest or motion.
s = displacementu = initial vel.a = accelerationt = time
Velocity–Displacement
v² = u² + 2as
Relates velocity and displacement without time.
v = final vel.u = initial vel.a = accelerations = displacement
Newton's 2nd Law
F = ma
Net force equals mass times acceleration.
F = force (N)m = mass (kg)a = acceleration (m/s²)
Weight
W = mg
Gravitational force on an object near Earth's surface.
W = weight (N)m = massg ≈ 9.81 m/s²
Friction
f = μN
Frictional force between two surfaces in contact.
μ = coefficient of frictionN = normal force
Kinetic Energy
KE = ½mv²
Energy possessed by a body due to its motion.
m = massv = velocity
Potential Energy
PE = mgh
Gravitational potential energy relative to a reference height.
m = massg = gravityh = height
Work Done
W = Fd·cosθ
Work done by a force over a displacement at angle θ.
F = forced = displacementθ = angle
Power
P = W/t = Fv
Rate of doing work; also force times velocity.
P = power (W)W = work (J)t = time
Momentum
p = mv
Product of mass and velocity; conserved in closed systems.
p = momentumm = massv = velocity
Impulse
J = FΔt = Δp
Change in momentum equals force times time interval.
J = impulseF = forceΔt = time interval

Torque

Spring Force (Hooke's Law)

Restoring force of a spring proportional to extension.

Elastic PE (Spring)

Energy stored in a stretched or compressed spring.
k = spring constantx = extension

Thermodynamics Heat · Temperature · Entropy · Gases
Ideal Gas Law
PV = nRT
Relates pressure, volume, moles, and temperature of an ideal gas.
P = pressureV = volumen = molesR = 8.314 J/mol·K
First Law of Thermodynamics
ΔU = Q − W
Change in internal energy equals heat added minus work done by system.
ΔU = internal energyQ = heatW = work
Heat Transfer (Conduction)
Q = mcΔT
Heat gained/lost by a substance during temperature change.
m = massc = specific heatΔT = temp. change
Thermal Conductivity
Q/t = kAΔT/d
Rate of heat flow through a material of thickness d.
k = conductivityA = aread = thickness
Efficiency of Heat Engine
η = 1 − T꜀/T_H
Carnot efficiency between hot and cold reservoirs.
T_H = hot temp.T꜀ = cold temp. (K)
Entropy Change
ΔS = Q/T
Entropy change in a reversible process at temperature T.
ΔS = entropy changeQ = heatT = temperature (K)
Stefan–Boltzmann Law
P = εσAT⁴
Power radiated by a blackbody surface.
ε = emissivityσ = 5.67×10⁻⁸ W/m²K⁴A = area
Thermal Expansion
ΔL = αL₀ΔT
Linear expansion of a solid due to temperature rise.
α = expansion coeff.L₀ = original length

Waves & Sound Oscillations · Wave Properties · Acoustics
Wave Speed
v = fλ
Wave speed equals frequency times wavelength.
v = speedf = frequency (Hz)λ = wavelength (m)
Period & Frequency
T = 1/f
Period is the reciprocal of frequency.
T = period (s)f = frequency (Hz)
Simple Pendulum Period
T = 2π√(L/g)
Period of oscillation of a simple pendulum of length L.
L = lengthg = gravity
Spring-Mass Period
T = 2π√(m/k)
Period of a mass oscillating on a spring.
m = massk = spring constant
Doppler Effect
f' = f(v ± v_o)/(v ∓ v_s)
Observed frequency shift when source or observer moves.
v = wave speedv_o = observer speedv_s = source speed
Sound Intensity Level
β = 10 log(I/I₀)
Decibel scale relative to threshold of hearing I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m².
β = dB levelI = intensityI₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m²
Standing Waves (String)

Wavelengths of harmonics on a string fixed at both ends.

Angular Frequency

Angular frequency relating cycles to radians per second.
ω = angular freq.f = frequencyT = period

Newton's Law of Gravitation

Gravitational attractive force between two masses.

g = GM/r²
Field strength at distance r from a mass M.

Orbital Speed
v = √(GM/r)
Speed needed for circular orbit at radius r.

T = periodr = orbital radiusM = central mass

Modern Physics Quantum · Photoelectric · de Broglie · Bohr
Planck's Equation
E = hf = hc/λ
Energy of a photon in terms of frequency or wavelength.
h = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·sf = frequencyc = 3×10⁸ m/s
Photoelectric Effect
KE_max = hf − φ
Maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons; φ = work function.
h = Planck const.f = frequencyφ = work function
de Broglie Wavelength
λ = h/p = h/mv
Wave nature of matter; wavelength associated with moving particle.
h = Planck const.p = momentum
Heisenberg Uncertainty
ΔxΔp ≥ ℏ/2
Fundamental limit on simultaneous precision of position and momentum.
ℏ = h/2πΔx = pos. uncertaintyΔp = mom. uncertainty
Bohr Radius
rₙ = n²a₀
Orbital radius of hydrogen electron at quantum number n.
a₀ = 0.529 Ån = principal quantum no.
Hydrogen Energy Levels
Eₙ = −13.6/n² eV
Energy of electron in nth energy level of hydrogen atom.
n = principal quantum no.1 eV = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J
Mass-Energy Equivalence
E = mc²
Rest mass energy; fundamental result of special relativity.
m = massc = 3×10⁸ m/s
Compton Scattering
Δλ = (h/mₑc)(1 − cosθ)
Wavelength shift when photon scatters off an electron.
h = Planck const.mₑ = electron massθ = scatter angle

γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²)
Relativistic factor; approaches ∞ as v → c.
v = relative velocityc = speed of light
Time Dilation
Δt = γΔt₀
Moving clocks run slow relative to a stationary observer.
Δt₀ = proper timeγ = Lorentz factor
Length Contraction
L = L₀/γ
Moving objects appear shorter along direction of motion.
L₀ = proper lengthγ = Lorentz factor
Relativistic Momentum
p = γmv
Momentum of a particle increases with velocity relativistically.
γ = Lorentz factorm = rest massv = velocity
Relativistic Energy
E = γmc²
Total relativistic energy of a moving particle.
γ = Lorentz factorm = rest mass
Energy–Momentum Relation
E² = (pc)² + (mc²)²
Fundamental relativistic relation; for photon: E = pc.
p = momentumm = rest massc = light speed
